I Ditched Social Media

Patrick August 27, 2025

As a blogger, it was once a standard practice to republish blog posts across the entire social media landscape. Besides RSS, it was recommended to be active on Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and other such gated platforms. I deleted Facebook and Twitter many years ago. It's been so long, I can't even recall when. Although I hated Facebook's takeover of Instagram and the new features added to compete with Snapchat and TikTok, I kept it and endured years of enshittification. There was still some worth in following small bike builders and other cycling-related accounts.

But "following" is dead today. Instagram's algorithms have pushed the content of accounts you follow out of your feed. Instead, you get sponsored ads and content that is supposed to keep you engaged. The stuff that you came for? Intentionally invisible! My Instagram feed wasn't even as bad because I never bothered with stories and didn't watch reels. I went there for photo posts. That's what Instagram originally offered, and that's the only thing I wanted. But the quality has degraded to the point where Instagram has lost all meaningful value.

I considered ditching it for a year, now. When my wife and I went on a two-week trip through Italy, I took no photos, and I never opened the Instagram app. When we returned, I occasionally opened the app and was bored with it. So, at the end of July, I nuked my account with permanent deletion scheduled for August 31st. I also had a Bluesky account, which I cancelled on August 24th. Bluesky is fairly new, smaller, and friendlier. But I'm done with social media. It's never been a healthy place to spend your time. Not for me; not for anyone.

I've been active on Strava, the social network for athletes, since August 2011. The activity feed, which displays the activities of people you follow and the challenges they join, exposes you to one of the negative aspects of such platforms – social comparison. When you're wage-slaving in an office from 7 to 5 and you see that others are seemingly always out riding, it's bound to make you feel envious. Damn, you suck. You're working from the crack of dawn to the fall of dusk to put food on the table while others enjoy daytime rides. I've turned off notifications from Strava's app for that reason. When I'm stuck in the daily grind, I don't want my phone to tell me that others are out having fun. "Following" is dead there, too. These days, I seldom add someone new to follow, and I rarely receive follow requests. The social element of Strava appears to be gradually disappearing, and it may not be a bad thing.
