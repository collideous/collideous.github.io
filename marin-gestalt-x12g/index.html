Marin Gestalt X12G

Patrick June 15, 2025

When you read a press release about a new road or gravel bike, do they ever talk about geometry? Hardly ever. The latest and greatest road bike is promoted as faster, stiffer, and more aerodynamic than its predecessor. Most marketing is race-focused and speed-obsessed. Claimed reductions in aerodynamic drag and the watts saved, as a result, are things that a weekend warrior like myself cannot verify and will not notice while riding. When companies issue such press releases to online bike publications, I greet them with a massive yawn. The bike industry is turning wrong by focusing on race-only bikes for World Tour and domestic professionals, ignoring that most cyclists don't race.

Gravel bikes, in particular, serve as "do-it-all" bikes for most. Yet somehow, the bikes on which Pogačar wins the Tour and van der Poel wins the Gravel WC should be the bikes I want to ride. Tell you what, a 56-year-old dude with a 7-to-5 job can't do anything worse for his health and enjoyment than riding a bike built for a 26-year-old professional athlete. Strangely, many seem to think what's good for a pro is good for them. The aerodynamic advantages that save Pogačar a handful of seconds up the Mont Ventoux are meaningless for Joe Average. What truly matters is how a bike feels and how it handles. It's utterly unimportant if I can ride a particular loop 0.05 km/h faster. What matters, however, is how I feel when I get home. Am I beaten up because the frame is too stiff, or do I come back with a smile because the frame is designed for comfort?

While steel is hailed for its vibration-eating comfort and carbon is praised for its race-winning stiffness, tube shapes, diameters, and wall thicknesses are just as important as the material of a frame. Above all is how the single tubes that make up a bike frame are sized to form the geometry of a frame. Whereas mountain bikers chew over stack and reach, head and seat tube angles, or fork offset and trail, it seems to me that such things don't seem to garner a lot of interest in road or gravel circles. It should, though. Bike geometry defines how the rider sits on the bike, where the center of gravity of the rider and bike are, and how the unit of the two will behave while moving. Geometry defines how a bike handles uphill and downhill, how it turns or copes with crosswinds, how stable it rolls on the flat, and how easily it sprints.

I bought a Gestalt X10 in the spring of 2023 and made my X12B out of it. Other than Evil's Chamois Hagar, no road/all-road/gravel bikes are on the market with such a "progressive" geometry. I've already put 6,188.0 km on my X12B. To ensure thousands more miles on this excellent platform, the X12G was born. It shares a lot of the same parts as the X12B, with a few notable differences. It shifts with a SRAM Red derailleur instead of Rival. The saddle and seatpost differ. While the X12B is equipped with René Herse Antelope Hill 700C x 55mm tires, the new X12G has narrower 48mm Hatcher Pass tires.

2023 Vs. 2025 Marin Gestalt X10

Besides the obvious color change, the 2025 Marin Gestalt X10 has undergone slight changes from the 2023 model. The microSHIFT Advent X groupset was replaced by Sword, and the blackwall Vee Tire Rocket Man tires were swapped with skinwall 700Cx45 Quickstyx tires. Since I only used frame, fork, headset, and thru-axles, let's take a look at what changed between 2023 and 2025. The biggest change is the fork. It's an entirely new fork. It is beefier, and the fork legs have a more triangular shape. The thread in the fork crown to attach a fender moved from the front to the underside of the crown. The steerer tube of the 2023 fork had a 45° shoulder and doesn't require a race, while the 2025 fork uses one. Personally, I prefer the more rounded shape of the old fork.
