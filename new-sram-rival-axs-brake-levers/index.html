New SRAM Rival AXS Brake Levers

Patrick August 1, 2025

A package with two SRAM Rival boxes was dropped off at offtrail.guru headquarters. Here's the scoop. The SRAM Red AXS hoods that were initially on my Nordest Albarda were transferred to my Ritte Snob Disc, from which I stole them to build my 2025 Marin Gestalt X12G. They will be going back to the Ritte at the end of the year. New 2025 SRAM Force AXS hoods are going to take their place. To keep the X12B and X12G similar, I decided to replace the 2-year-old Rival hoods (model ID ED-RIV-D1) with brand-spanking-new brake levers (model ID ED-RIV-E1).

The new design isn't a rebadged version of what came before. The all-new hoods are longer and slimmer. There's enough room to wrap all four fingers and the thumb around the hood. SRAM claims it provides a better grip, resulting in less hand fatigue. The biggest improvement comes from the redesigned brake levers. SRAM claims 80% less effort when braking from hoods, and 33% less effort when braking from the drops. The new levers have a higher pivot point that's farther forward. SRAM switched to a "push piston" design, similar to how mountain bike brakes work. This simplifies the internals, provides a more direct hose installation without requiring a second wrench, and reduces a significant amount of weight.

They're definitely a step forward. I like the shape and feel. To differentiate the old Rival levers from Force and Red, SRAM used LFRT (long-fiber-reinforced thermoplastic) brake levers. They had a stealth matte and gloss-black finish. The new ones are a step up and come with carbon brake levers like their higher-end Force and Red siblings. You get bonus buttons that provide an auxiliary shift option or ANT+ device control with Force and Red. I don't use any devices on my bike, but additional thumb-shifters would be handy. The more affordable Rival levers forgo that extra feature. The grey "Rival" logotype matches the grey of the rear derailleur — a thumbs-up for that.

I purchased the levers together with the calipers, which I'm not using. They're said to be stiffer. They're noticeably lighter and cool-looking. I'll be doing the same with my X12G at the end of the year, but on this bike, the calipers will be used. The installation was done in three days:

  • Day 1 to take off the old levers and install the new ones
  • Day 2 to wrap the bars
  • Day 3 to bleed the brakes

That's just how I like to get things done — no need to do it all at once in one marathon session.
