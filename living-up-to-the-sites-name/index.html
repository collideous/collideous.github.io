Living Up To The Site's Name

Patrick January 20, 2025

The past two weekends I lived up to the name of this blog. I fat-biked off-trail. On Sunday, January 12th I took my fat-bike to Les Prés d'Orvin for a loop to the Petit Chasseral. The snow conditions were spectacular. The weather however wasn't. It was foggy and a cold wind was blowing. I don't mind that. When I'm out fat-biking on snow, the snow quality is all that matters. The days ahead of the 12th were warm with rainfall. On Friday, the mercury started dropping and went well below zero on the weekend. That gave the snow a nice, hard crust thick enough to carry a cyclist and his fat-bike. This is the absolute best snow a fat-biker can wish for. There’s no need to follow trails of any kind. That's what I love, and that's what I did. The only thing that strong winds and fog don't offer is opportunities to shoot good photos.

The weekend of January 18th and 19th had it all: good snow and a blue sky. I rode for over three hours and 26 km on Saturday, and nearly 4 hours and 28 km on Sunday. Most of the kilometers I rode were off-trail. The wind and weather were perfect for using my DJI Neo. I took it out of my backpack frequently and let it follow me. The video footage isn't something I'd post on YouTube or elsewhere. It's not like I have the skills of a Danny MacAskill or a Brandon Semenuk, and who really wants to watch an old guy ride his fat-bike across the snow? The videos captured dozens of great photos, which I quickly extracted before dinner at home. I uploaded over sixty pictures from those two rides to Flickr.

Until last weekend, I used the DJI Fly app on my iPhone to control the Neo. When the Neo follows me, it’s nice to glance down at the phone to check what the Neo is doing. There’s one major disadvantage, though. Keeping the DJI Fly app running for an extended period drains the battery. So this weekend, I left my iPhone in the pocket of my jacket and used the quick-mode buttons on the Neo. I listened to the sounds and watched for the shadow to ensure it followed. Twice, the Neo couldn’t keep up. When that happens, the drone stops and hovers in place. I turned around and rode toward the Neo. As soon as it recognized me, it flew straight toward me and jumped back into follow mode. In a year or two, I’m definitely getting a faster drone. I’m sure DJI and HOVERAir will compete to produce even more capable self-flying drones. Until then, the Neo is more than good enough.
