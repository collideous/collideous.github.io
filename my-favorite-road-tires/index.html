My Favorite Road Tires

Patrick May 24, 2025

In the old days, road cyclists firmly believed that tires needed to be narrow and inflated to ridiculous pressures to be fast. 25 mm wide tires used to be the de facto standard. In more recent times, many warmed up to 28 mm tires. While René Herse has been testing and comparing much wider tires for many years, bike publications have only very recently discovered that wide tires don't slow you down. Cyclingnews now says that "40mm road tires are faster for nearly everyone".

I didn't have to wait for them to tell me something I'd known for almost a decade. I set up my red Volagi Viaje road bike with Compass Barlow Pass tires in 2016 and never looked back. Since then, I've ridden almost every tire width between 38 and 55 mm in 650B and 700C. Ironically, the largest of them are also the ones I like most. In 650B, that's the 48 mm (52mm on my rims) Panaracer Gravelking slick tires. In 700C, René Herse's 55 mm wide Antelope Hill tires are my all-time favorites.

We cyclists hate when something slows us down. A narrow tire that wasn't adequately inflated or lost air or a brake that was rubbing, we quickly complained when our bike was holding us back. I never felt that way when using ever wider tires. While I don't have the data to say that a tire made me faster or slower, I can definitively say that wider tires add comfort. The fresher I stay on the bike, the longer I can ride and the easier I can get back on the bike the next day.

With the switch to wide tires came the switch to tubeless. I set up all the above tires tubeless using Orange Seal sealant and a Schwalbe tire booster. I haven't had a flat tire since then. The only disadvantages are the need to add sealant once in a while, the time it takes to clean the rims, and changing rim tapes when changing tires.

Back in 2011, I used Eclipse TPU tubes for my 29er. TPU tubes have made a huge comeback and I'm ready to give them another go. TPU tubes are lighter than butyl and latex tubes and lighter than tubeless sealant. They're also faster, more puncture-resistant, and can easily be patched. Lastly, they pack minimal. TPU tubes are incompatible with CO2 inflators, but I don't use those. René Herse now offers the best of both worlds with a TPU tube sealant — you get the convenience of tubes with the flat-proofness of tubeless. Eclipse does as well!

My recent online purchases around tires:

  • René Herse Antelope Hill tires, 700C x 55 mm, standard tan
  • René Herse Hatcher Pass tires, 700C x 48 mm, standard tan
  • René Herse TPU tubes, 700C x 45-68 mm
  • WTB NanoAir TPU tubes, 700C x 32-47 mm
  • Ridenow TPU tubes, 700C/29" 56G, 45 mm Presta
  • Ridenow TPU tubes, 700C/29" 45G, 45 mm Presta
  • Ridenow TPU tubes, 700C/29" 45G, 65 mm Presta
  • Eclipse TPU sealant

The Hatcher Pass tires will be set up with René Herse TPU tubes and Eclipse TPU sealant. I rode my previous Antelope Hill tires for 5,342.8 km with Orange Seal tubeless milk and never had a flat. Let's see if TPU tubes are as reliable as well.
