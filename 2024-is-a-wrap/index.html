2024 Is A Wrap

Patrick December 31, 2024

Although I’m far from where I was in 2017/18, I logged more rides in 2024 than in the past two years. All the numbers, time spent in the saddle, overall distance, and total elevation climbed are up. I spent 75.6% of my riding time on the road, 17.2% on the fat bike, and only 7.2% mountain biking. Ideally, I’d like to share an equal amount of time for each activity type. Yesterday, I uploaded my 10,000th photo to Flickr, most of which are cycling-related. What’s next?

  • logging an all-time distance of 80,150 km on Strava
  • logging 5,000 all-time hours
  • logging 2,500,000 m all-time elevation gain, and after that, 300 Everests
  • reaching my 2,000th activity

Not all of the above has to be achieved next year, just whenever is fine with me. The end of the year allowed me to fat bike a lot right from home with no money spent on gas and parking. If I don’t have to pollute our planet and waste time traveling, I prefer that. Weekends are short enough already. What I don’t spend behind the wheel, I can spend behind a handlebar. Last week, that was over 15 hours behind the Jones bar of my Snakedriver.

No new bike joined my stable in 2024, but all bikes saw updates except for my Stooge MK5. I blogged about the changes to my bikes throughout the year. I replaced the 150 mm Thomson Covert dropper post on my 44 Snakedriver in early November with a 185 mm BikeYoke Revive 3.0 dropper post. I also treated the bike to a Chris King seatpost clamp matching the gold King headset and bottom bracket.

I spent 2024 on three bikes: the Marin Gestalt X12, the Stooge MK5, and the 44 Snakedriver. I’m considering selling my Ritte frameset and its wheels in spring 2025. I’d move the Red AXS brake/shift levers and the carbon Rotor Aldhu cranks from it to my Volagi Viaje. The leftover parts will be transferred to a new bike I’d like to assemble sometime during 2025. The Ritte is a jaw-droppingly gorgeous road bike. It’s just that I’ve moved on, and traditional road bikes with skinny tires are no longer my thing. Nothing’s set in stone yet, though.

Lastly, I bought a DJI Neo in September, which I use for the photos posted here. I use the Neo's videos to extract snapshots from the recordings. I marvel at how well that little drone can follow me each time I watch the videos. It's intelligent enough to recognize branches and other obstacles that get in the way. I help the drone, though, by picking my lines based on the safest path for the drone. When there are a lot of branches hanging across the trail, I also slow down so that the Neo has more time to figure out the best flight path. I uploaded a recent video to Flickr if you're curious how the Neo can follow a cyclist through the woods. It was the best purchase of the year, period.

I’ll never buy a bike with fully internally-routed cables, for instance. I just won’t. But I love options. And options we have. Besides, when you can take an 11-year-old Volagi Viaje and throw the very newest components on it, where’s the reason to moan and groan? Standards haven’t changed all that much. You just need to be smart about the bikes and components you’re choosing.
