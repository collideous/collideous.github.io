Saying Goodbye To Flickr After 20 Years

Patrick November 14, 2025

A few months back, I ditched the few social media apps I had been using.  I had never been the doomscroller. Facebook and Instagram were places to visit when the feeds showed posts in chronological order from the people I was following. Once algorithms fed me a constant flow of garbage, I started hating these apps. Interestingly, for instance, Instagram's mobile app was far worse than its web app. Since most users visit these platforms on mobile devices, that's where most advertising dollars are going. None of these platforms have anything worthwhile to see these days. I banned them from my phone, and I don't miss them a bit.

Shortly after wiping them off my phone, I also deleted the two news apps I was using – Google News and Feedly. They were useful, but if I can read the news when I'm sitting at a computer, why waste time doing the same on the phone? This reduced my daily iPhone screen time to about 20 minutes. Something funny happens when you only look at your phone for 15 to 20 minutes a day. You no longer feel the need to buy a new phone. My small iPhone 12 Mini is perfect for the handful of things I use it for. It'll only be replaced once it can no longer run the latest iOS.

The app and service cleanse continued. I sunsetted my Flickr account. I joined in 2006 – a whopping 20 years ago – and uploaded 10,509 photos. Flickr used to be a pretty vibrant community, but like all other social media, user activity has largely disappeared. I'm as guilty as anyone. I don't check what anyone else is uploading. Until 2018, a Pro account cost $24.95 and has climbed to $79.99 annually for the past four years. It will be $89.00 in 2026. Add subscriptions to Strava, iCloud, Netflix, Office 365, etc., and a chunk of hard-earned cash is missing from your checking account. The closer I get to retirement, the more I want to cut such monthly expenses. Landline, cable TV, and car ownership could one day end up on the same chopping block.

I didn't delete my Flickr account in one go. First, I cancelled my Pro subscription. When you do that, Flickr offers you a lower subscription. I declined the offer. My Pro subscription was paid for until June 2026, and I could have kept my account until then. I have all those photos in Apple Photos and backed up in iCloud. It was time to pull them from public view.  Deleting my account was shockingly easy – a few clicks and it was gone, no confirmation email whatsoever. It is my goal to leave no traces online and to wipe all the data I've left here and there, including this website. Two web profiles remain for now – LinkedIn and Strava.
