The P-Brakes That Came Years Before V-Brakes

Patrick December 14, 2024

The first bike components I designed and manufactured were a set of linear-pull cantilever MTB brakes. I designed them in the last year of my apprenticeship and CNC-machined them during summer break in my first year of college. The apprentice department where I did my apprenticeship let me use their machines. I made four tensioner nuts, washers, spring covers, brass sleeves, and steel bushings on a conventional Schäublin 102 tabletop lathe. The lever arms and a fixture to make them were machined on a CNC vertical mill - a Mikron WF31C with a Heidenhain TNC155 control. I worked on those and the larger WF51C during my apprenticeship. I loved working on those and was good at programming them.

Mechanical Explanation of the P-Brakes

The internal tension spring design was similar to Dia Compe's 986 cantilever brakes. Each arm had an adjustable internal tension spring. One end of the spring fit into a hole in the arm, the other end into an aluminum hex nut used to adjust the spring tension. An aluminum sleeve covered the spring assembly, leaving just the hex bottom visible. A brass sleeve was pressed into the brake arm and the spring cover. 180º opposite from the hole for the spring was a second through-hole. It housed a spring dowel pin that held everything together. This 3-piece assembly revolved around a steel bushing. This bushing had a second function. When tightening the M6 button head mounting bolt, the steel bushing clamped the hex nut to maintain the spring tension. I made a large aluminum washer placed in front of the arm. This was purely aesthetic.

The pad-holding assembly came from Shimano cantilever brakes from that time. It wasn't the easiest to adjust. It was an assembly that was readily available and easy to incorporate. It included an eyebolt-type pad holder, a wedge-shaped washer, and behind the arm, a convex and concave washer, a thin washer, and a steel hex nut. The wedge-shaped washer was used to set the pad "toe-in.”

V-Brakes by Shimano or Avid had a cable bridge at the top of the right arm that held the noodle and a left arm with a cable clamp. The arms of my P-Brakes were identical. The cable bridge and macaroni-sized cable guide I designed was a welded unit. You had to bend the tip of a brake cable to push it through the macaroni. Since no cable clamp was incorporated into any arm, I used an adjustable cable barrel clamp. This design allowed me to place the macaroni on either side, which gave me more freedom in routing my brake cables. A Teflon liner inside the macaroni reduced friction.

I made a set of hubs later on. As a cyclist with an engineering background, ideas for bike components have popped up over the years. But early into my career, I moved away from manufacturing. Making stuff isn’t impossible without personal connections to people in manufacturing or being in a company with manufacturing capabilities. A machine shop will make anything you want if you hand them a big pile of cash. But, whenever I asked myself if it was worth spending a small fortune to bring a silly idea to life or build a bike with components already on the market, I chose the latter. My disposable income has always allowed me to own very nice bikes, but made-to-my-spec parts were out of reach.
