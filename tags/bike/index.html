2023 Vs. 2025 Marin Gestalt X10

Besides the obvious color change, the 2025 Marin Gestalt X10 has undergone slight changes from the 2023 model. The microSHIFT Advent X groupset was replaced by Sword, and the blackwall Vee Tire Rocket Man tires were swapped with skinwall 700Cx45 Quickstyx tires. Since I only used frame, fork, headset, and thru-axles, let's take a look at what changed between 2023 and 2025. The biggest change is the fork. It's an entirely new fork. It is beefier, and the fork legs have a more triangular shape. The thread in the fork crown to attach a fender moved from the front to the underside of the crown. The steerer tube of the 2023 fork had a 45° shoulder and doesn't require a race, while the 2025 fork uses one. Personally, I prefer the more rounded shape of the old fork.

Marin Gestalt X12G

When you read a press release about a new road or gravel bike, do they ever talk about geometry? Hardly ever. The latest and greatest road bike is promoted as faster, stiffer, and more aerodynamic than its predecessor. Most marketing is race-focused and speed-obsessed. Claimed reductions in aerodynamic drag and the watts saved, as a result, are things that a weekend warrior like myself cannot verify and will not notice while riding. When companies issue such press releases to online bike publications, I greet them with a massive yawn. The bike industry is turning wrong by focusing on race-only bikes for World Tour and domestic professionals, ignoring that most cyclists don't race.

Possibly My Last Bike Purchase

I recently bought a second Marin Gestalt X10 to build my X12G. The X12G is very similar to my 2023 X12B. I set it up with several on-hand parts, both used and new, and a bunch of newly purchased ones, just like I always do. I turn 56 this year, and the Gestalt X12G is possibly the last bike I built. I have a fleet of seven complete bikes and an incomplete road bike, the Ritte Snob Disc. There's a fat-bike, a single-speed, and a 27.5+ geared MTB for trail riding. Then, there are four drop-bar bikes for road and gravel. All of them can be ridden for several more years. If I rebuild the Ritte Snob Disc and ride each of my drop-bar bikes for 3 years, that's 15 years. I'll be 71 years old.

Marin Gestalt X12 On New Wheels

How wide is too wide? My wide tire journey for road cycling started on Compass Barlow Pass (700x38) tires back in 2016. WTB Horizon (650x47) followed in 2018. When I put 650B wheels on my Nordest Albarda, René Herse Switchback Hill (650x48) and Panaracer GravelKing (650x48, 52mm actual) tires took me a big step further on that journey. Volagi Viaje 2024 Update

Following in the footsteps of the 1x12 conversion of my Nordest Albarda, the Volagi Viaje naturally has to be taken there, too. Volagi brought this bike to market in a Kickstarter campaign in August 2012. It was designed for the bike components of that time. Whenever new stuff comes out, message boards and comment sections fill up with cyclists moaning and groaning about the bike industry, forcing new standards upon us. I’m not one of them. I love seeing bike technology advance. I don’t adopt everything. In fact, a lot that’s supposedly “forced” on us isn’t my cup of tea. I’ll never buy a bike with fully internally-routed cables, for instance. I just won’t. But I love options. And options we have. Besides, when you can take an 11-year-old Volagi Viaje and throw the very newest components on it, where’s the reason to moan and groan? Standards haven’t changed all that much. You just need to be smart about the bikes and components you’re choosing.
